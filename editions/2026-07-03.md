### Stories considered
- **National Grid Ventures to invest $1.75B for a 35% stake in Joulent — chosen for the news post.** Announced Tuesday 1 July (PR Newswire; SEC Form 6-K), National Grid plc's commercial arm, National Grid Ventures, agreed to invest $1.75 billion for a 35% interest in Joulent LLC, a US power-development platform serving large-load customers such as data centres and AI. The deal values Joulent at ~$5 billion and is incremental to National Grid's existing ~£70bn (~$90bn) five-year capital plan. Joulent's flagship is Project Kilby, a 2.67-gigawatt gas-fired plant in West Texas that will supply a Microsoft-operated data-centre campus under a 20-year power-purchase agreement, developed with Chevron and using GE Vernova turbines, targeting first power in 2028. The strategic hook: Joulent's "Across-the-Meter" model co-locates generation at the data-centre fence line so electricity flows from turbine to server without touching the public grid — a deliberate bypass of the interconnection queues now bottlenecking AI build-out. This is the freshest, hardest, most money-moving AI-finance beat in the window, and it opens a new rung on the week's "how the AI build-out is financed" ladder: the physical power layer the capital has been chasing. The post sticks to the claims the release and filing support — the $1.75B investment, the 35% stake, the $5B valuation, and Joulent building co-located generation for AI data centres that bypasses the public grid. Project Kilby / Microsoft / Chevron detail left to the Notes to keep the post tight.

### Pattern context
This closes a week whose single thread — how the AI build-out gets financed — climbed one rung of the capital stack each day: company capital markets (SK Hynix, 29 Jun), the whole M&A market (Bain, 30 Jun), sovereign industrial policy (South Korea, 1 Jul), and a purpose-built sovereign-linked fund (MGX, 2 Jul). Friday's National Grid / Joulent deal turns the camera from who is paying to what the money is actually short of: power. The "Across-the-Meter" bypass of the public grid is the tell — the binding constraint on AI has quietly moved from dollars to megawatts and interconnection queues. Recurring names (Microsoft, the hyperscalers, the memory-chip complex) now recur inside a power-infrastructure deal rather than a chip listing or a fund close.
