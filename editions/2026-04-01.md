── DEEP DIVE ───────────────────────────
The Anthropic ruling deserves a closer read because it redraws the map for AI companies navigating government contracts. Judge Lin didn't just block a procurement action — she established that the government cannot weaponise supply chain risk designations to punish companies for taking public ethical positions. That's a constitutional firewall that didn't exist a month ago.

The second-order effects are substantial. Defence contractors who had begun scrubbing Claude from their workflows can now reverse course. Anthropic's $14B annualised revenue — much of it from enterprise API contracts that touch government-adjacent work — is no longer under existential threat. The amicus briefs from OpenAI employees, Google's Jeff Dean, Microsoft, and retired military leaders signal that the entire AI industry views the Pentagon's move as a line-crossing moment.

But the ruling also clarifies the trade-off. OpenAI signed its Pentagon deal hours after Anthropic was blacklisted, accepting the "all lawful purposes" terms Anthropic rejected. The market now has two distinct models: OpenAI's unrestricted government access versus Anthropic's ethical red lines backed by judicial protection. For institutional investors evaluating these companies ahead of potential IPOs, the question is whether Anthropic's stance costs it revenue or earns it a premium. The judge's ruling suggests the legal system will protect the right to choose — but the market will still price the consequences.

The companies most affected aren't the AI labs. They're the defence primes — Lockheed, Raytheon, Northrop — who now need to build AI strategies knowing that their choice of model provider carries First Amendment implications. That's a compliance complexity nobody had on their procurement checklist six months ago.

The bottleneck narrative progressed from GPUs (3/18) to CPUs (3/26) to memory (3/24, 3/27) to energy (3/31) — and today Samsung's $73B bet confirms that even the suppliers see the constraint as structural, not cyclical. Meanwhile, the government-AI relationship is fracturing along ethical lines: the SEC embedded AI governance into standing examinations (3/28), the CFTC is building advisory infrastructure, and now a federal court has drawn a constitutional boundary around the Pentagon's ability to coerce AI companies. The Great Rotation (3/27) pulled capital out of AI equities, but private markets kept pouring in — SoftBank's $40B loan, Physical Intelligence at $11B, Broadcom guiding up. The divergence between public scepticism and private conviction is the widest it's been all year.

── ONE CONTRARIAN THOUGHT ──────────────
The IPO withdrawals are being read as a risk-off signal for AI. I think they're actually bullish for the private AI companies in the pipeline. Every month the IPO window stays shut, OpenAI, Anthropic, Plaid, and Revolut accumulate more revenue, more users, and more leverage over their eventual public offering terms. SoftBank didn't structure a 12-month bridge loan because it's worried about the IPO market — it structured it because a delayed listing at a higher valuation is worth more than a rushed one into a choppy tape. The companies pulling IPOs today aren't the strong ones. The strong ones are choosing to wait.
