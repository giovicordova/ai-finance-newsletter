AI FINANCE BRIEFING — 31 Mar 2026
═══════════════════════════════════════════

SoftBank borrows $40 billion to fund its OpenAI bet — the largest dollar-denominated loan in the company's history, with a 12-month fuse that points straight at an IPO.

── FIVE THINGS ─────────────────────────
1. **SoftBank takes on $40B bridge loan for OpenAI stake** — The loan, arranged by JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Mizuho, SMBC, and MUFG, matures March 2027 and funds SoftBank's $30B commitment to OpenAI's $110B raise. The 12-month term maps almost exactly onto market expectations for an OpenAI IPO window, and the lending syndicate's willingness to underwrite it unsecured tells you the banks see that listing as a near-certainty.
   → https://money.usnews.com/investing/news/articles/2026-03-27/softbank-secures-40-billion-loan-to-fund-further-openai-investment

2. **S&P Global warns $635B in AI capex faces energy shock** — Big Tech's planned infrastructure spend for 2026 — $200B from Amazon, $175–185B from Google, $135B from Meta — now faces a geopolitical stress test. The Iran conflict has disrupted Middle East helium supply (one-third of global output), a critical input for chip manufacturing, and persistently high oil prices could force capex revisions as early as Q2.
   → https://finance.yahoo.com/sectors/technology/articles/big-techs-635-billion-ai-062102915.html

3. **Fink's annual letter reframes AI's binding constraint as energy, not chips** — BlackRock's CEO told investors that data centre power demand will rise 165% by 2030 to 945 TWh — equivalent to Japan's total electricity consumption. He warned AI risks concentrating wealth further unless investment access expands, while quietly omitting that BlackRock's own HLEND private credit fund gated redemptions at 5% earlier this month when requests hit 9.3%.
   → https://www.blackrock.com/corporate/investor-relations/larry-fink-annual-chairmans-letter

4. **Physical Intelligence in talks for $1B at $11B valuation** — The two-year-old robotics AI startup, founded by ex-DeepMind and Stanford researchers, would double its valuation in four months. Founders Fund is set to lead, with Lightspeed, Thrive Capital, and Lux returning. The deal signals that AI capital is now flowing aggressively into physical-world applications, not just software.
   → https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-27/ex-deepmind-staffers-robotics-startup-in-talks-for-11-billion-valuation

5. **Agentic AI hits 42% production deployment — but 60% of enterprises lack governance** — BCG sizes the agentic AI services opportunity at $200B, with 91% of CXOs planning budget increases in 2026. Banking and financial services lead adoption. The catch: six in ten organisations deploying agents have no formal governance framework, and data readiness remains the top blocker at 58%.
   → https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/the-200-billion-dollar-ai-opportunity-in-tech-services

── DEEP DIVE ───────────────────────────
Fink's letter and S&P Global's warning mark an inflection in how the market should price AI infrastructure risk. For two years, the narrative was chip scarcity — GPUs, TSMC waitlists, custom silicon. That story isn't wrong, but it's becoming secondary.

Energy is now the binding constraint, and it carries a fundamentally different risk profile. Chip supply is a manufacturing problem with capital solutions. Energy is a physics-and-politics problem. Data centres consumed 415 TWh globally in 2024 and are on track to double by 2026. US residential electricity prices have risen 36% since 2020, and PJM is projecting a 6-gigawatt shortfall by 2027.

The winners are not obvious. NVIDIA's dominance is priced in. What's less reflected is value accruing to power infrastructure — utilities with spare capacity, on-site generation developers, and companies like Lumentum (which just took $2B from NVIDIA for optical interconnects that reduce power per bit). The losers: hyperscalers building in grid-constrained regions, and any AI startup whose unit economics assume cheap, abundant electricity.

Fink is talking his book — BlackRock wants to be the AI era's infrastructure capital allocator — but the thesis is sound. The $635B capex plan assumed stable energy markets. That assumption is under pressure from geopolitics and domestic grid constraints. Watch for the first hyperscaler to revise capex guidance downward — that's when energy moves from narrative risk to P&L risk.

── TRAJECTORY ──────────────────────────
Three threads from the last two weeks are converging into one story. The infrastructure bottleneck narrative broadened from GPUs (3/18) to CPUs (3/26) to networking (3/27) to energy (today) — the constraint is now the full stack, not any single component. Regulatory posture continued its shift from frameworks to enforcement, with the SEC making AI governance a standing examination priority (3/28) and CFTC now building its own AI advisory apparatus. The adoption-versus-governance gap we flagged last week (Gartner's 40% cancellation forecast on 3/27) now has a sharper number: 60% of enterprises deploying agentic AI have no formal governance. The pattern is clear — deployment is outrunning the guardrails, and the correction will come from regulators, not from the market.

── ONE CONTRARIAN THOUGHT ──────────────
Everyone is focused on whether AI capex is sustainable. The more interesting question is whether it's sufficient. The $635B hyperscaler spend sounds enormous until you measure it against Fink's energy math: powering 945 TWh of data centre demand by 2030 requires not just GPUs but power plants, transmission lines, and grid upgrades that take 5–10 years to build. The AI industry is not overspending on compute. It is dramatically underspending on energy infrastructure. The companies that figure this out first — and start vertically integrating power — will define the next decade. The rest will be capacity-constrained by physics, not by capital.
