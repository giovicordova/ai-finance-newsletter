── DEEP DIVE ───────────────────────────
The Q1 funding numbers deserve more than a headline because they reveal a market that is concentrating at a speed that should make both bulls and bears uncomfortable.

Four companies raised $188B — 65% of all global venture capital in a single quarter. That level of concentration has no precedent in the history of private markets. OpenAI alone absorbed $122B, more than the entire global VC market deployed in any quarter before 2024. The top four rounds accounted for more capital than the bottom 5,996 startups combined.

The bull case writes itself: frontier AI requires frontier capital, and these companies are the only ones with the models, the talent, and the distribution to justify the cheques. The bear case is subtler. When 80% of venture capital flows into a single sector, every other sector gets starved. Fintech, biotech, climate — they're competing for the 20% that's left. The downstream effect is a funding environment where non-AI startups face a capital drought even as headline numbers scream abundance.

For financial services specifically, the concentration creates a dependency risk the Fed flagged explicitly two weeks ago. If OpenAI and Anthropic together capture over half of all frontier AI investment, and banks are building mission-critical workflows on their APIs, then venture capital allocation decisions are effectively shaping systemic financial infrastructure. That's a governance gap nobody is talking about.

The other signal buried in the data: Q1 venture funding equalled 70% of all 2025 full-year investment. Either Q2–Q4 sustains this pace — implying over $1T in annual VC deployment, a figure that strains credulity — or the quarter was an anomaly inflated by a handful of mega-rounds that won't repeat. If the latter, the cooling will be sudden and the startups that raised at Q1 valuations will face a mark-to-market reckoning by year-end.

══ WEEKLY INTELLIGENCE ═════════════════
**Week of 28 March – 3 April 2026: The Week Capital Spoke Louder Than Markets**

This was the week where the disconnect between public market sentiment and private market conviction became impossible to ignore — and the AI infrastructure story shifted from building compute to building everything around it.

The dominant theme was capital commitment at unprecedented scale, moving in opposite directions depending on where you looked. Public equity investors continued the Great Rotation that began in late March, with Microsoft still down 20% YTD and the broader AI trade under pressure. But private markets delivered the most emphatic counter-argument imaginable: $297B in Q1 venture funding, $242B of it flowing to AI, four mega-rounds totalling $188B. SoftBank borrowed $40B unsecured to fund its OpenAI stake (3/31), a bet so large it required five banks to underwrite. The lending syndicate's willingness to do so without collateral tells you more about institutional conviction than any stock price.

The infrastructure narrative evolved from "we need more GPUs" to "we need more of everything." Samsung committed $73B in 2026 capex to reclaim the memory chip crown from SK Hynix (4/1). Crusoe secured 12 GWh of iron-air batteries from Form Energy, explicitly building power independence alongside compute. Vertiv's 64% YTD rally and $15B backlog confirmed that thermal management and power delivery are now the fastest-growing segments of data centre economics — not an afterthought, but the main event. BlackRock's Fink warned that data centre power demand will hit 945 TWh by 2030, equivalent to Japan's total electricity consumption (3/31). The constraint stack is now GPU → CPU → memory → networking → energy → cooling, and each layer is binding simultaneously.

Regulation continued its shift from framework to enforcement. The SEC embedded AI governance into standing examination priorities (3/28), the CFTC convened its Innovation Advisory Committee on AI risk management for derivatives, and Commissioner Peirce's upcoming SIFMA speech on AI agent liability signals that Q2 will bring the first wave of enforcement actions targeting firms whose AI governance doesn't match their AI deployment. The Anthropic ruling (4/1) — where a federal judge blocked the Pentagon's supply chain blacklisting as First Amendment retaliation — added a constitutional dimension that nobody had on their regulatory bingo card.

The open-source counter-offensive gained real momentum. Google's Gemma 4 launched under Apache 2.0 on April 2, delivering a 31B-parameter model with no commercial restrictions. This is strategically significant because it attacks the pricing power of proprietary API providers at exactly the moment enterprises are looking for cost discipline. The timing — during a public market rotation away from AI — could accelerate enterprise adoption of open models as CFOs look for ways to maintain AI capabilities while cutting vendor spend.

What actually matters in three months: the Q1 earnings season starting mid-April will be the first real test of whether $650B in hyperscaler capex is generating measurable returns. Microsoft, Alphabet, and Meta all report in late April. If AI revenue breakouts show acceleration, the Great Rotation reverses. If they disappoint, the capital drought in public AI equities deepens — even as private markets keep pouring in. The structural shift to watch is whether the open-model movement (Gemma 4, Llama variants) starts showing up in enterprise procurement decisions. The pricing power thesis that supports OpenAI's $840B valuation and Anthropic's $60B depends on enterprises staying on proprietary APIs. Every Apache 2.0 release chips away at that assumption.

Position for next week: all eyes on ISM Services PMI Monday for early AI productivity signals, and the March jobs report Friday for evidence that AI deployment is reshaping labour demand in financial services. The real action starts April 24 when Microsoft and Alphabet report simultaneously — that's the day the market gets its answer on AI capex conversion.
