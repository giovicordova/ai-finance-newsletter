── DEEP DIVE ───────────────────────────
**The Great Rotation: what happens when the market gives up on AI before AI gives up on the market?**

The numbers are stark. Microsoft down 20% year-to-date. Nvidia off 7% in March. The S&P 500 dragged lower by its own concentration in semiconductors and software. The capital isn't disappearing — it's moving into industrials, energy, and financials.

The irony is the rotation is happening precisely as AI deployment metrics hit all-time highs. Hedge fund adoption reached 100% this week. Enterprise agentic AI projects are surging. Hyperscalers have committed $650B in 2026 capex. The market isn't arguing that AI doesn't work — it's arguing that the capex-to-revenue conversion is taking too long.

This creates an unusual setup. The companies actually generating AI application revenue — Anthropic at $14B annualised, OpenAI north of $25B — are still private. Public investors are stuck paying for the infrastructure layer without direct access to the application revenue that infrastructure is building. CoreWeave's underperformance versus Nebius is instructive: both are burning cash to build AI factories, but the market is now differentiating on capital efficiency, not top-line growth.

The winners in this rotation are infrastructure companies with locked-in contracts and clear margin trajectories. The losers are firms whose AI narratives outran their AI revenue. Memory makers like SK Hynix and Samsung sit in an unusual sweet spot — they're old-economy cyclicals that happen to be direct AI beneficiaries, which means the rotation may actually lift them.

The risk the market is underpricing: if the rotation forces AI capex discipline, the companies that survive it emerge with better unit economics. Forced efficiency often creates better businesses than unlimited capital.

── TRAJECTORY ──────────────────────────
The past ten editions tell a story of escalation meeting resistance. The infrastructure bottleneck broadened from GPUs (March 18) to CPUs (March 26) to memory (today) — every layer of the stack is now a constraint. Regulatory activity shifted from publishing frameworks to active enforcement across four agencies in five days. And now the market itself is pushing back: the Great Rotation marks the first time capital flows have turned explicitly against AI equities even as deployment metrics accelerate. The gap between adoption velocity and investor patience is the defining tension of Q1 2026.

── ONE CONTRARIAN THOUGHT ──────────────
The Great Rotation might be the best thing that happens to AI in 2026. Two years of unlimited capital created a generation of AI companies with no pricing discipline and no urgency to demonstrate unit economics. If the public market rotation forces hyperscalers to justify their capex and startups to prove margins, the survivors will build businesses that actually compound — not just businesses that raise. The correction isn't a verdict on the technology. It's a verdict on the business models, and that's a distinction worth a lot of money.

**Week of 20–27 March 2026: The Week AI Met Gravity**

This was the week the AI trade hit a wall — not because the technology failed, but because the market ran out of patience with the gap between infrastructure spending and revenue proof.

**The infrastructure story broadened into a full-stack crisis.** Monday opened with AI-driven trading hitting 35% of US equity volume (March 23), a milestone that underscored how deeply embedded AI already is in financial plumbing. But the supply chain told a harder story. Micron's HBM revenue tripled to $3.1B (March 24), confirming the memory bottleneck is tightening, not easing. By Wednesday, NVIDIA's post-GTC enterprise orders had surged 50% as Vera Rubin pre-orders opened (March 25). Thursday brought the revelation that CPUs — not GPUs — are emerging as the chokepoint for agentic AI, with AMD and Intel warning of six-month lead times and 10%+ price hikes (March 26). By Friday, memory spot prices had spiked 700% year-over-year. The bottleneck isn't a single component anymore — it's systemic, spanning silicon, memory, networking, and power.

**Regulators made a coordinated move.** The week saw a concentrated burst of supervisory action that went well beyond routine. The FDIC issued consumer lending AI guidance (March 24). The Federal Reserve published its AI impact assessment (March 25). The Senate Banking Committee grilled bank CIOs on AI risk (March 25). The SEC established a permanent AI Task Force (March 26). Four agencies, four substantive actions, five days. The message is unambiguous: AI is now financial infrastructure, and it will be supervised with the same rigour as any other systemically important technology. The shift from "we're watching" to "we're examining" happened this week.

**Adoption hit 100% — and that's where the hard questions start.** Hedge fund AI adoption reached universal baseline status (March 26), meaning zero holdouts remain. But beneath that headline, a quality gap is widening. Citadel is building a 100-person dedicated AI research lab in London (March 24). Numerai crossed $5B AUM (March 25). Meanwhile, the broader industry is deploying off-the-shelf tools with minimal customisation. The gap between best-in-class and baseline is growing even as adoption converges — and Gartner's projection that 40% of enterprise agentic AI projects will be cancelled by end of 2027 suggests the industry is running ahead of its governance and execution capacity.

**Then the market blinked.** By Friday, the Great Rotation was the dominant narrative: institutional capital pouring out of AI mega-caps into old-economy cyclicals. Microsoft down 20% year-to-date. Nvidia off 7% in March. The irony is acute — deployment is at all-time highs, but the market is punishing the infrastructure layer for spending faster than it can monetise. The highest-conviction AI bulls are now private market investors backing OpenAI at $840B and Anthropic at $60B+, while public markets are pricing in scepticism.

**What connects the week:** The collision between AI's industrial scaling phase and the market's demand for proof. The technology is embedding itself into financial infrastructure at an unprecedented rate — 35% of equity trading, 100% hedge fund adoption, permanent regulatory oversight across four agencies. But the companies building that infrastructure are being valued as if the cycle is peaking, not beginning. That disconnect — accelerating deployment against decelerating investor patience — is the defining tension heading into Q2.

**Forward look:** CoreWeave's one-year anniversary on March 28 will invite fresh scrutiny of AI infrastructure unit economics. Q1 earnings season begins in April, and the first cloud and semiconductor reports will either validate or break the rotation thesis. The single most important metric to watch: AI revenue as a percentage of total capex. Any company that can demonstrate meaningful AI revenue at positive margins will be the counterexample the market desperately needs. Until someone proves the conversion math, the rotation has room to run.
