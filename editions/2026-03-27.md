The gap between ambition and execution is widening, not narrowing.

── DEEP DIVE ───────────────────────────
The Gartner forecast deserves attention precisely because it runs against the prevailing narrative. Every earnings call, every survey, every consulting report says AI adoption is accelerating — and that's true. But adoption and success are different measurements. A 40% cancellation rate for agentic AI projects means enterprise budgets are being allocated faster than organisations can absorb the technology.

The pattern is familiar from previous enterprise tech waves: cloud migration in 2015-2018 saw similar failure rates before stabilising. The difference with agentic AI is the governance burden. Financial regulators — the SEC, FINRA, the BoE — are all independently arriving at the same conclusion: AI systems require the same oversight as any material business tool. FINRA's 2026 report explicitly flags that firms using GenAI must modify their supervisory procedures to account for AI-specific risks.

For financial institutions, this means the real cost of agentic AI isn't the compute or the models — it's the compliance infrastructure. The winners won't be the fastest deployers. They'll be the ones who built governance frameworks that let them deploy without triggering regulatory tripwires. JPMorgan's early investment in agentic AI for internal tasks like pitch deck generation looks shrewd in this light: low-risk use cases that build institutional muscle before regulators tighten the screws on client-facing applications.

── TRAJECTORY ──────────────────────────
Three threads have dominated the last seven editions. First, the infrastructure bottleneck keeps broadening: GPUs were the constraint in week one, then CPUs emerged as the agentic AI chokepoint, HBM memory tightened further, and now networking and power management are repricing. The supply chain story is no longer about any single component — it's systemic. Second, the regulatory drumbeat is accelerating: the SEC AI Task Force, FINRA's GenAI governance report, the BoE's stress testing framework, and the FDIC's lending guidance all landed within days of each other. The direction is unmistakable — treat AI like any other material business system. Third, there's a growing tension between adoption universality and deployment quality. Hedge fund AI adoption hit 100%, enterprise agentic AI projects are surging, but Gartner's 40% failure prediction suggests the industry is running ahead of its governance and execution capacity.

── ONE CONTRARIAN THOUGHT ──────────────
Everyone's focused on OpenAI's $840B valuation, but the more interesting number is $20B in annualised revenue against a valuation that implies 42x revenue. For comparison, Microsoft trades at roughly 14x revenue with $81.3B quarterly. The gap only makes sense if you believe OpenAI will dominate enterprise AI revenue for a decade — and the Fed just flagged concentration in model providers as a systemic risk. A future where regulators push for model provider diversification is a future where OpenAI's pricing power compresses. That $840B assumes a monopoly that regulators have explicitly said they don't want to exist.
