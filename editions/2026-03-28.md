── DEEP DIVE ───────────────────────────
The AI revenue surge deserves scrutiny because it reveals who's actually paying for the trillion-dollar buildout. Anthropic's $14B annualised revenue and OpenAI's $20B-plus both come overwhelmingly from enterprise contracts. CIOs are footing the bill, and the window to negotiate favourable terms is closing fast.

The pattern mirrors previous enterprise technology cycles — mainframes, databases, cloud — where heavy capital investment was followed by rapid adoption and a small number of dominant winners. But the current cycle has a twist: the pricing power is shifting to vendors faster than in any prior wave. OpenAI's 42x revenue multiple and Anthropic's 1,000% growth rate reflect a market where customers are locking in capacity before competitors do, not because the ROI case is proven.

NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang's suggestion that AI projects shouldn't be judged by near-term ROI drew jeers, but his underlying point — that compute access is becoming a strategic asset — is harder to dismiss when hyperscalers are committing $650B in a single year. The enterprises that secured AI infrastructure early are the ones that can deploy at scale. Everyone else is joining a queue.

For financial services specifically, the cost calculus is more complex. The SEC's 2026 examination priorities now treat AI governance as a standing requirement, not a special topic. Every AI tool that touches client money, compliance, or investor communications needs documented governance. That compliance layer adds cost that the AI vendors' pricing models don't account for.

── TRAJECTORY ──────────────────────────
Three threads have defined the past ten editions. The infrastructure story has expanded from a GPU shortage into a full-stack constraint spanning CPUs, HBM memory, networking, and power — every link in the data centre chain is now a bottleneck, not just silicon. The regulatory trajectory has shifted from announcements to enforcement: the SEC, FINRA, BoE, and FDIC have all moved from publishing frameworks to actively examining against them. And the adoption-versus-quality tension has crystallised — 100% hedge fund adoption, 72% of Global 2000 deploying agents, but Gartner's 40% cancellation forecast suggests the industry is spending far faster than it's learning to govern. The common thread: AI infrastructure is becoming as systemically important as financial infrastructure, and regulators are treating it accordingly.

── ONE CONTRARIAN THOUGHT ──────────────
The conventional wisdom is that AI vendor pricing power will compress as competition intensifies. I think the opposite happens for at least two more years. Anthropic, OpenAI, and the hyperscalers are the only ones with the capital and compute to train frontier models, and enterprises are embedding these APIs into mission-critical workflows that are expensive to rip out. The switching costs are building faster than the competitive alternatives. By the time open-source models close the capability gap, the lock-in will be structural. The time to negotiate was 2025. In 2026, you're paying list price.

══ WEEKLY INTELLIGENCE ═════════════════
This was the week AI finance stopped being a technology story and became an infrastructure story. The distinction matters because it changes who wins, what gets funded, and how regulators think about the sector.

The dominant theme was supply chain broadening. When we started the week, the GPU shortage was the headline constraint. By Friday, that narrative had expanded into something more systemic. NVIDIA's Vera Rubin platform launched with seven chips — GPUs, CPUs, inference accelerators, networking, and storage — because no single component is the bottleneck anymore. The CPU supply crisis emerged as the week's most underappreciated development: six-month delivery lead times, prices up 10%, and AMD's data centre head calling demand increases "unprecedented." Meta deploying four custom MTIA chips to reduce NVIDIA dependency confirmed that even the largest buyers are worried about concentration risk in their hardware supply chains. Micron's HBM revenue tripling to $3.1B with capacity sold out through 2027 completed the picture: every component in the AI stack is constrained, from silicon to memory to networking to power.

The regulatory drumbeat accelerated from framework to enforcement. The SEC AI Task Force, FINRA's GenAI governance report, the BoE's stress testing framework, and the FDIC's lending guidance all became operational this month. The SEC's 2026 examination priorities embed AI oversight into virtually every examination — not as a standalone category but woven into cybersecurity, fiduciary duty, and operational resiliency reviews. For financial institutions, this means AI governance is no longer a compliance project. It's a standing operational requirement, and the first wave of AI-focused examination letters is expected to hit investment advisers in Q2.

The funding picture told two stories simultaneously. On one hand, capital is flooding in at record scale: OpenAI's $110B round at $840B valuation, hyperscalers committing $650B in 2026 capex, fintech funding rebounding 27% to $51.8B. On the other, the market is bifurcating sharply — AI companies attract easy funding at premium valuations while traditional fintechs struggle, deal counts dropped 23% even as dollar volumes rose, and Gartner's 40% agentic AI project cancellation forecast suggests much of the spending is running ahead of execution capacity.

The structural shift to watch going into next week: AI is transitioning from a tool companies use to infrastructure companies depend on. OpenAI and Anthropic are becoming systemically important providers — the Fed flagged concentration risk explicitly, and regulators across jurisdictions are independently arriving at the same conclusion. The financial services firms that built governance frameworks early — JPMorgan's low-risk agentic deployments, the European bank consortium for shared compliance models — are the ones positioned to deploy without triggering regulatory tripwires. Everyone else is building the plane while flying it, and the 40% cancellation rate suggests a lot of those planes won't land.

Position for next week: Q1 earnings season approaches, and the market will demand evidence that $650B in AI capex is generating returns, not just revenue for AI vendors. Watch for any CEO who shifts language from "AI investment" to "AI ROI" — that's the narrative inflection point that separates this cycle from a bubble.
