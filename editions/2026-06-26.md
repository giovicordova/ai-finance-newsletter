### Stories considered
- **Micron's 16 multi-year Strategic Customer Agreements — chosen for the news post.** On its fiscal Q3 2026 earnings call (after the U.S. close on 24 June), Micron Technology disclosed that it has signed 16 long-term "take-or-pay" contracts with customers across data-centre, consumer and automotive markets. The contracts run roughly three to five years, lock in binding purchase volumes, and carry about $22 billion in customer financial commitments (~$18B in cash deposits plus ~$4B in letters of credit). Fourteen of the 16 carry cumulative minimum-price revenue of roughly $100 billion over their terms; Micron expects that once all such agreements are in place, half or more of total company revenue will sit under them. This is the distinct, genuinely-fresh, money-moving disclosure of the session: not yesterday's record-revenue headline but the forward demand lock-in that drove the ~9–15% post-earnings reaction on 24–25 June and the wave of analyst commentary that the contracts "reset how memory is valued" by attacking the sector's core cyclicality fear. It is also the direct rebuttal to Tuesday's KOSPI crash, which fell on exactly the worry these contracts address — whether AI-hardware demand is durable. The news post sticks to claims the chosen CNBC report supports — 16 multi-year contracts, the three-to-five-year terms, the $22 billion in customer commitments, and the half-or-more-of-future-revenue expectation — and deliberately omits the $100 billion minimum-revenue figure (sourced to the call/secondary outlets, not clearly in the CNBC piece), the deposit/letter-of-credit split, the gross-margin floor mechanics, and the share-price move, to keep every claim verifiable from the single link. https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/24/micron-mu-earnings-report-q3-2026.html

### Pattern context
The month's dominant thread has been how the AI compute build-out is financed and who ultimately books the revenue. This week the thread played out as a question and its answer. Monday, the Nasdaq-100 mechanically pulled four AI-infrastructure names (CoreWeave, Nebius, Astera Labs, Teradyne) into the index passive funds must hold. Tuesday, Dell and Super Micro Computer turned Nvidia's next-generation Vera Rubin chips into booked server revenue, with Dell named builder of a U.S. government flagship supercomputer. Then the reckoning: South Korea's KOSPI fell 8.1% and tripped circuit breakers twice on the fear that hyperscalers cannot sustain AI-hardware spending. Thursday and Friday delivered the rebuttal — Micron's record $41.5B quarter, and today the forward half: 16 multi-year, take-or-pay customer contracts worth ~$22B in commitments (~$100B in minimum revenue) that lock the demand in. Qualcomm's Dragonfly roadmap, with Meta as a named customer, widened the merchant-silicon field beyond Nvidia in the same window. Recurring names: Micron, SK Hynix, Samsung, Nvidia, Qualcomm, the AI-infrastructure complex.
