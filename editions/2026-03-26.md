── DEEP DIVE ───────────────────────────
The CPU supply crisis is the most underappreciated story in AI infrastructure right now. Everyone's been watching GPU allocation like hawks, but the shift to agentic AI — where autonomous bots orchestrate across multiple agents, move data between systems, browse the web, and pull from databases — is hammering general-purpose compute in ways the market hasn't priced.

NVIDIA's Jensen Huang mentioned agentic AI a dozen times on the last earnings call. The company's response: break the Vera CPU out as a standalone chip and build dedicated CPU-only racks with 256 liquid-cooled chips. Meta is already deploying NVIDIA Grace CPUs at scale and evaluating Vera for its data centers. This is NVIDIA entering Intel and AMD's territory directly.

The financial implications ripple outward. AMD warns lead times are at six months. Intel expects inventory to hit its "lowest level" this quarter. For cloud providers and banks building agentic AI infrastructure, this means procurement timelines just doubled. Any enterprise that hasn't already locked in CPU supply for its 2026 AI deployments may find itself waiting until 2027.

The winners: NVIDIA (expanding TAM into CPUs), AMD (pricing power on constrained supply), and any infrastructure provider that secured capacity early. The losers: enterprises that assumed GPUs were the only bottleneck, and anyone planning agentic AI deployments without a CPU procurement strategy.

Baseline adoption is table stakes. Differentiated deployment is the only moat.
