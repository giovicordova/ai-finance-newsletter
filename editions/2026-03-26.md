## Deep Dive

The CPU shortage is this week's most underappreciated story. For two years, the AI hardware narrative centred on GPUs — who could buy them, who couldn't, what they cost. That trained the market to think of AI infrastructure as a single-component problem. It isn't.

An AI server needs GPUs, yes, but also high-bandwidth memory (Micron's HBM sold out through 2027, per last Tuesday's earnings), CPUs to orchestrate workloads, power infrastructure to keep everything running, and advanced packaging to connect it all. Each layer is now constrained.

Intel's 6-month lead times stem from fabrication yield problems at its own plants. AMD's delays come from TSMC prioritising GPU production over CPU orders. The result: companies that spent months securing GPU allocations now face a second procurement queue for a component that used to arrive in two weeks.

This has direct implications for the $450B in planned AI datacenter capex this year. If CPU and memory shortages push server deployment timelines out by 3–6 months, the revenue payoff from all that capital investment gets delayed correspondingly. Cloud providers promising AI capacity to enterprise clients — including the financial services firms racing to deploy — may have to revise delivery dates.

The winners are the companies with vertically integrated supply chains. Apple, Amazon (via Graviton), and Google (via TPUs with custom CPUs) are less exposed. Everyone relying on Intel and AMD for server CPUs is in a queue that just got longer.

## Trajectory

Nine editions in, a clear infrastructure pattern emerges: the bottleneck story has climbed the component stack from GPUs (edition 1 — spot prices falling 30%) to HBM memory (edition 7 — Micron sold out through 2027) and now to CPUs. Meanwhile, regulatory intensity has accelerated on a parallel track — SEC enforcement, FDIC guidance, BoE stress tests, a Senate hearing, a Fed assessment, and now Treasury launching a dedicated initiative, all within nine trading days. The infrastructure squeeze and the regulatory net are tightening simultaneously. Financial services firms face the worst of both: they can't build fast enough, and the rules for what they're building keep expanding.

## One Contrarian Thought

Wall Street is modelling AI infrastructure spend based on GPU procurement — $600B from the Big Five, $1T in NVIDIA orders through 2027. But if CPUs, memory, packaging, and power are all binding constraints simultaneously, the actual deployment timeline stretches well beyond what the capex announcements imply. The market is pricing the spend but not the bottlenecks. That gap between committed capex and operational capacity is where the next earnings disappointment will come from.
