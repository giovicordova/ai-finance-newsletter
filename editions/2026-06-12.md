### Stories considered
- SpaceX prices the largest IPO in history — chosen for news post. SpaceX confirmed the pricing of its initial public offering of 555,555,555 shares of Class A common stock at $135 each on Thursday 11 June 2026 after the US close (Europe/Rome overnight), raising roughly $75 billion — the largest IPO on record, easily eclipsing Saudi Aramco's $29.4 billion 2019 listing. The pricing implies a valuation of about $1.75–1.77 trillion, placing SpaceX among the ten largest listed companies in the world and roughly the seventh-largest US-listed company. The all-primary offering means all proceeds flow to the company. Shares begin trading on the Nasdaq Global Select Market on Friday 12 June 2026 under the ticker "SPCX" (the debut trade itself happens after the US open, i.e. after this morning's Europe/Rome briefing window — so the printable beat is the pricing, not the first-day pop). The AI-finance hook: SpaceX absorbed Elon Musk's AI company xAI earlier in 2026 (the February merger valued the combined entity at ~$1.25 trillion), and the prospectus states proceeds fund continued growth of its AI compute infrastructure (Colossus / Colossus II in Memphis and Mississippi, ~1 GW combined), launch facilities and vehicles, and the Starlink satellite constellation; SpaceX also plans orbital AI data centres and its own AI chips. The AI segment is now ~17% of revenue and ~61% of FY25 capex ($12.7B of $20.7B) per the S-1. This is the capital-markets event that closes the week and the thread every Mon–Thu edition flagged as "Friday's beat". ### Pattern context
Friday closes a week whose single most important AI-finance story was structural rather than a one-off: the financing of AI compute fragmented into at least five parallel mechanisms, each of which moves the chip-capex burden off the model lab's own equity. Monday framed the political-capital route (Trump–OpenAI public-stake talks + OpenAI's confidential S-1); Tuesday the op-ex-licence route (Apple leasing Google Gemini for Siri AI); Wednesday the private-credit route (Broadcom / Apollo / Blackstone's $35B Anthropic SPV with a chip-vendor residual-value backstop); Thursday the customer-prepayment route (Oracle's $638B backlog with $75B of customer-funded GPUs); and Friday the public-equity route (SpaceX's record $75B IPO, now an AI conglomerate after absorbing xAI). The SpaceX listing is also the cleanest single illustration of the week's compute-as-asset theme: it is simultaneously a launch company, a satellite operator and — post-xAI — an AI-compute landlord renting ~110,000 GPUs to Google for $920M/month and Colossus capacity to Anthropic for $1.25B/month.
